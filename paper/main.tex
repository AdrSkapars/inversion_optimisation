\section{Introduction}

As frontier language models are deployed into increasingly agentic settings, the worst-case behaviour they will exhibit on the long tail of \emph{natural} inputs --- realistic user turns, not engineered adversarial strings --- matters more than their average-case behaviour on a static benchmark~\citep{jones2025forecasting}.
This is a different problem from adversarial robustness: jailbreak attacks deliberately push the target out of distribution to elicit harmful outputs, whereas the question we care about is how often a rare-but-realistic input pattern at deployment elicits an undesired behaviour.
A model that resists every gibberish-suffix jailbreak can still be reliably nudged into sycophancy, persona drift, or unprovoked insults by ordinary-looking conversation, and those are the failures users will actually see.
Automated behavioural evaluation pipelines such as BLOOM~\citep{gupta2025bloom} have made probing for these failures scalable: an LLM evaluator generates scenarios from a behaviour description, converses with the target model, and a judge scores the transcripts.
Such pipelines are appealing because they are reproducible, scoped to a named behaviour, and require no human-in-the-loop after specification.

Two limitations remain.
First, the rollout is purely black-box.
The evaluator runs a single trajectory per scenario, picking a message from its own reasoning and the target's text reply, and never sees which of several phrasings of its message would have moved the target further towards the behaviour.
Casper et al.~\citep{casper2024blackbox} argue that this kind of black-box auditing produces only lower bounds on worst-case behaviour, and that any signal from target-model internals strictly tightens these bounds; concurrent work on adaptive evaluation generation~\citep{brown2025task,feng2025dataswarms} likewise treats the target as an oracle.
Second, the existing methods that \emph{do} read target internals come from a different lineage entirely.
Token-level adversarial attacks such as BEAST~\citep{sadasivan2024fast} and GCG~\citep{zou2023universal} optimise inputs against target log-probabilities, but with no awareness of the evaluation scaffold: their outputs are either gibberish suffixes or fluent-but-off-scenario text, and they branch only over inputs --- never over the target's responses.
They also operate in the adversarial-robustness regime --- engineered prompts no real user would type --- rather than the natural-input regime that determines deployment behaviour, so even when they succeed they say little about how the model will fail in production.

We close this gap by recasting each behavioural rollout as a search problem over a tree of possible interactions, branching on \emph{both} sides of the conversation.
On the input side, we draw evaluator messages from BLOOM's existing deliberative context (strategy, scenario, open message tag), but search over them at three granularities --- token, sequence, and turn --- scored by the target's log-probability of a hypothesised behaviour-eliciting response start.
On the output side, we use a contrastive proposal distribution --- adapted from contrastive decoding~\citep{li2023contrastive,liu2021dexperts} as a red-teaming mechanism --- that biases target-side sampling towards regions where bad continuations are concentrated, while still reporting scores under the target's own distribution.
The whole loop is closed by a model-driven refiner that observes previous transcripts and judgement scores and produces an updated evaluator strategy for the next round.
Figure~\ref{fig:method} shows the resulting tree-search rollout end-to-end; Section~\ref{sec:method} gives the full mechanics.

Empirically, the combined pipeline dominates every existing method on the main-comparison slice across elicitation, fluency, on-topic-ness, and diversity, while preserving the variance and reproducibility properties of BLOOM.
Refined strategies transfer only weakly across target models, indicating that the gains are genuinely model-specific rather than universal jailbreak templates.
Among tree-search policies, the cache-friendly rollout-and-restart variant wins the compute-vs-elicitation frontier, suggesting that more elaborate tree exploration does not pay back the KV-cache locality it forfeits.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Context-conditioned target-aware input search} at token, sequence, and turn granularity, scored by target log-$P(\textrm{TRS})$ inside BLOOM's deliberative context --- eliminating the gibberish/off-scenario failure modes of context-free attacks like BEAST.
  \item \textbf{Contrastive guided output sampling.} We adapt contrastive decoding as a sample-efficient proposal for finding high-likelihood undesired target outputs, extending the sampling-at-attack-time line~\citep{beyer2026sampling} to multi-turn rollouts while reporting scores under the target's own distribution.
  \item \textbf{Rollouts as a two-sided tree search} with model-driven refinement, comparing rollout-and-restart, beam, MCTS, and best-first turn-level policies and reporting KV-cache reuse as a first-class compute metric.
  \item \textbf{Headline result + applied evaluations.} Our pipeline outperforms BLOOM, BEAST, \PH{BoN}, \PH{PAIR}, and FLRT at matched compute on elicitation, fluency, on-topic-ness, and diversity, with per-component ablations showing each layer pulls its weight; applied across five target models and six BLOOM behaviours, with weakly transferring strategies confirming the gains are genuinely model-specific.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}

% \emph{Stub.} Position with respect to (i) automated red-teaming and behavioural elicitation pipelines including BLOOM and concurrent multi-stage evaluator frameworks; (ii) token-level adversarial input search (GCG, BEAST, AutoDAN); (iii) contrastive decoding and logit arithmetic (Li et al.~2023, DEXPERTS, classifier-free guidance), which we adapt as a red-teaming output search; (iv) self-refinement and reflective agent loops; (v) tree-search and sequential Monte Carlo methods for controlled generation.


\paragraph{Automated behavioural evaluation.}
\citet{perez2022redteaming} introduced the LLM-evaluator-against-target paradigm, automating red-teaming by having one language model generate test inputs for another and a classifier score the resulting transcripts.
BLOOM~\citep{gupta2025bloom} formalises this into a four-stage pipeline (understanding, ideation, rollout, judgement) producing reproducible evaluation suites from a behaviour description.
Petri~\citep{fronsdal2025petri} takes a broader, more exploratory approach, equipping an auditor with tools to set system prompts, roll back conversations, and prefill target responses.
\citet{huang2025multiturn} benchmark static, offline, and online methods for multi-turn behaviour elicitation, finding that static test cases saturate within a year of curation while online methods continue to find failures efficiently.
All of these systems treat the target as a black box: the evaluator decides what to say from its own reasoning and the target's text responses, never from internal model signals.
We retain BLOOM's scaffold but replace the black-box rollout with a target-aware search scored by the target's own log-probabilities.

\paragraph{Adversarial input search.}
GCG~\citep{zou2023universal} finds adversarial suffixes via greedy coordinate gradients; BEAST~\citep{sadasivan2024fast} achieves comparable results with gradient-free beam search over tokens scored by target log-probabilities; AutoDAN~\citep{liu2023autodan} uses a genetic algorithm to evolve stealthier jailbreak prompts; FLRT~\citep{thompson2024flrt} pushes further on fluency by distilling against a toxified finetune of the target and adding a perplexity penalty.
On the prompt-iteration side, PAIR~\citep{chao2023jailbreaking} uses an attacker LLM to refine prompts from target feedback, and TAP~\citep{mehrotra2023tap} extends PAIR with branch-and-prune tree exploration over attacker prompts --- the closest prior work to our tree-search framing.
These methods optimise for the target but are disconnected from any evaluation context: suffixes are gibberish (GCG), fluent but off-scenario (BEAST, FLRT), or context-free attack templates (PAIR, TAP), because nothing anchors them to a scenario or behaviour specification.
Our context-conditioned sampling generates candidates inside the evaluator's full deliberative context (strategy, scenario, open message tag), so the search inherits topical framing automatically.

\paragraph{Self-refinement and reflection.}
Cross-round refinement in our pipeline borrows from a line of work in which a model critiques and revises its own outputs across rounds.
Self-Refine~\citep{madaan2023selfrefine} iterates a single LLM as generator and critic until the critic stops finding issues; Reflexion~\citep{shinn2023reflexion} adds an external memory of verbalised reflections that conditions the next attempt.
Our refiner conditions on prior transcripts and judge scores rather than self-evaluation, but the structural pattern --- observe past failures, emit an updated strategy, retry --- is the same.

\paragraph{Multi-turn jailbreaks.}
Crescendo~\citep{russinovich2024crescendo} elicits harmful responses through a sequence of innocuous-looking turns that gradually escalate, exploiting the same multi-turn structure we operate in.
Many-shot jailbreaking~\citep{anil2024manyshot} shows that simply lengthening the prompt with many demonstrations of compliance scales attack success log-linearly with context length.
Both are evidence that multi-turn dynamics are where behavioural failures actually live, motivating our turn-level search granularity and the turn-count scaling sweep in Table~\ref{tab:scaling}.

\paragraph{Contrastive decoding.}
Contrastive decoding~\citep{li2023contrastive} improves generation quality by subtracting amateur-model logits from expert-model logits; DExperts~\citep{liu2021dexperts} extends this for controllable generation with expert and anti-expert log-probability contributions.
We adapt the same principle as an \emph{output-side} red-teaming mechanism: the logit gap between a behaviour-eliciting copy of the target and the safety-tuned target serves as a proposal that steers sampling toward regions where undesirable continuations are concentrated.
Scores are reported under the target's own distribution, so the result is more efficient propensity estimation, not capability inflation.

\paragraph{Sampling at attack time.}
Closely related to our output-side contribution, \citet{beyer2026sampling} reframe adversarial attacks as a resource-allocation problem between prompt optimisation and repeated output sampling, showing that integrating sampling boosts success rates by up to 37\% and efficiency by up to two orders of magnitude over greedy-decoding-only attacks.
\citet{hughes2024bon} push this further with Best-of-N jailbreaking, achieving high attack-success rates almost entirely through scaled output sampling over minimally augmented prompts.
These sit within a broader literature on inference-time compute scaling~\citep{brown2024monkeys,snell2024scaling} showing that repeated sampling reliably trades compute for task success across domains.
Their settings are single-turn attack benchmarks with unguided i.i.d.\ output sampling; we extend the idea to multi-turn behavioural rollouts and replace unguided sampling with a contrastive proposal, while sharing the core insight that input and output search consume the same compute budget and should be allocated jointly.

\paragraph{Tree search for language.}
SMC steering~\citep{lew2023sequential} frames constrained generation as posterior inference with resampling at each token; tree-of-thoughts~\citep{yao2023tree} branches over reasoning paths.
We apply tree search to a different domain --- evaluation rollouts --- branching on both the evaluator's messages (input side) and the target's responses (output side), and comparing rollout-and-restart, beam, MCTS, and best-first policies on the compute-versus-elicitation frontier.

\paragraph{Why target-aware evaluation matters.}
Casper et al.~\citep{casper2024blackbox} argue that black-box auditing produces only lower bounds on worst-case behaviour and that white-box access yields strictly tighter estimates; sandbagging~\citep{vanderweij2025sandbagging} sharpens this point by showing frontier models can strategically underperform on evaluations.
\citet{jones2025forecasting} introduce the per-query \emph{elicitation probability} and show it scales predictably with query volume, so estimating it well at small scale forecasts deployment-scale failure rates --- our target log-$P(\text{TRS})$ scoring is a natural sample-efficient estimator of this quantity.
Concurrent work on adaptive evaluation generation either automatically discovers tasks where models fail~\citep{brown2025task} or optimises data generators toward desiderata such as difficulty and diversity~\citep{feng2025dataswarms}, but neither incorporates target-model internals into the generation process.
\citet{phuong2024frontier} likewise evaluate frontier models for dangerous capabilities through a fixed-suite black-box protocol --- exactly the regime our target-aware search is meant to tighten.


\section{Method}\label{sec:method}

\textbf{The BLOOM pipeline.} BLOOM runs four stages from a single behaviour seed (a short natural-language description, e.g.\ ``unprovoked-insults''): \emph{(1)~understanding} parses the seed into a structured behaviour specification; \emph{(2)~ideation} expands the specification into many concrete evaluation scenarios; \emph{(3)~rollout} has an evaluator LLM converse with the target on each scenario, producing a transcript; \emph{(4)~judgement} scores each transcript on elicitation and auxiliary qualities.
All target-aware search and refinement described above acts on stage (3); stages (1), (2), and (4) are reused unmodified, so the elicitation step only fires after planning is complete.

\emph{Stub.} Six subsections.
\textbf{Target-aware input search.} We sampled candidate user messages at three granularities --- token (BEAST-style beam), sequence (BoN-style), and turn (resample full turns and score after the target's reply) --- each scored by the target's log-probability of a hypothesised targeted response start (TRS).
\textbf{Context-conditioned sampling.} Every candidate was sampled inside the evaluator's full deliberative context (strategy, scenario, open $\langle$message$\rangle$ tag); the final message body was extracted post-hoc so the target saw a clean user turn.
\textbf{Contrastive guided output sampling.} On the target side we sample from the contrastive proposal $\log p_{\text{jailbroken}}(x_t \mid x_{<t}) - \alpha \log p_{\text{safe}}(x_t \mid x_{<t})$, then re-score the resulting rollouts under the target's own distribution using a multi-component objective: (i) judge-assessed output badness, (ii) target log-likelihood of the rollout, (iii) a behaviour-prior baseline rate, (iv) interaction naturalness (held-out perplexity).
$\alpha$ is a swept hyperparameter.
\textbf{Between-turn strategy generation.} Before each evaluator message, the evaluator emits a short tactical strategy block conditioning the next turn.
\textbf{Cross-round refinement.} A refiner observed previous transcripts and judgement scores, emitted an updated strategy, and that strategy was written back into the transcript metadata for the next round.
\textbf{Tree-search policy.} The default policy was rollout-and-restart (one full trajectory per round, refine, restart); we also ran beam-at-turn-level, MCTS-style, and best-first variants as ablations.

\paragraph{Baselines (parallel methods).} We compared against the following existing methods, run as standalone pipelines (not ablations of ours):
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{BLOOM}~\citep{gupta2025bloom} --- unmodified behavioural rollout, no target-aware search, no cross-round refinement.
  \item \textbf{BEAST}~\citep{sadasivan2024fast} --- token-level beam search over raw suffixes, without our evaluator-context wrapper.
  \item \PH{\textbf{BoN}} --- sequence-level Best-of-N, without our evaluator-context wrapper.
  \item \PH{\textbf{PAIR}}~\citep{chao2023jailbreaking} --- prompt-iteration attacker with no token-level search.
  \item \PH{\textbf{TAP}}~\citep{mehrotra2023tap} --- tree-of-attacks-with-pruning: PAIR extended with branch-and-prune exploration over attacker prompts. The closest existing baseline to our tree-search framing.
  \item \PH{\textbf{FLRT}}~\citep{thompson2024flrt} --- a fluent-attack improvement over GCG/BEAST that uses distillation from a toxified finetune of the target plus a perplexity penalty to keep generated prompts human-readable.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Component ablations of our method.} Each ablation knocks out or swaps a single component of our pipeline:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{$-$ Cross-round refinement} (single round only)
  \item \textbf{$-$ Between-turn strategy block}
  \item \textbf{$-$ Contrastive output sampling} (target decodes from its own logits, no jailbroken contrast)
  \item \textbf{Generate suffix vs.\ whole input.} The default samples a suffix attached to the evaluator's open $\langle$message$\rangle$ prefix; the ablation samples the whole input from scratch with no Phase-1 body carried over.
  \item \textbf{Token-stage resampling only} (BEAST-style, per-token scoring + beam search).
  \item \textbf{Sequence-stage resampling only} (BoN-style, score full sequences once and keep best).
  \item \textbf{Turn-stage resampling only} (resample whole user turns and score after observing the target's reply).
\end{itemize}
The full method combines all three resampling stages; mixed pairs (token + sequence with unscored filler, sequence + turn) are reported in the same table.

\paragraph{Input-side vs.\ output-side resampling.} A direct head-to-head between branching on the user's messages and branching on the target's responses, both treated as propensity estimators (output-side as a contrastively-biased proposal, then re-scored under the target's own distribution).
  We report (i) input-only (full method, contrastive proposal disabled), (ii) output-only (BLOOM rollout with contrastive output proposal on the target turns), (iii) both, and (iv) neither (BLOOM baseline).
  For the output-only and both conditions we sweep the contrastive weight $\alpha \in \{0.5, 1.0, 2.0\}$.
  To quantify sample efficiency we additionally report the number of target samples needed to recover a fixed top-$k$ set of highest-likelihood bad rollouts compared to unguided i.i.d.\ sampling.

\paragraph{Tree-search ablations.} These probe the framing of rollouts as tree exploration and the cost of breaking KV-cache locality:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Rollout-and-restart (default).} Run a full trajectory, refine strategy, restart.
      Maximises KV-cache reuse within a round.
  \item \textbf{Beam-at-turn-level.} Maintain a beam of top-$K$ conversation prefixes after each turn; the turn-level generalisation of BEAST's token-level beam.
  \item \textbf{MCTS-style turn search.} Build a tree of partial conversations with UCB-guided selection, rollout to a leaf, and backpropagate the judge score along the visited path.
      Terminal reward is the judgement score; internal-node value is the running mean of rollouts beneath it.
  \item \textbf{Best-first turn search.} Expand the partial conversation with the highest heuristic value next, using either the running judge score or target log-$P(\textrm{TRS})$ along the prefix as the heuristic.
      Cheaper than MCTS, no rollout-and-backprop.
\end{itemize}
We report both elicitation score and a KV-cache-reuse fraction for each variant. (Minimax / alpha-beta-style pruning is not applicable: the target has a fixed stochastic policy and is not an adversarial minimiser, so the pruning bound that makes alpha-beta efficient never fires.)

\subsection{Input-side vs.\ output-side resampling}

A head-to-head between branching on the user's messages and branching on the target's responses, in the spirit of the sampling-vs-optimisation budget split studied by \citet{beyer2026sampling} for single-turn attacks.
Both are propensity estimators: the output-side sampler uses a contrastively-biased \emph{proposal} but final scores are computed under the target's own distribution.
The relevant comparison is therefore sample efficiency at a fixed elicitation rate, not raw score gain.
